01 ‚Äî Research Objective

What does ELIS investigate?

The review is structured around a primary research question disaggregated into three analytical sub-questions that guide evidence extraction and synthesis.

Primary Research Question

What technological, operational, and institutional strategies have been shown to improve electoral integrity, and what does the empirical evidence reveal about their effectiveness?

1

Effectiveness: Which strategies demonstrate measurable improvements in electoral integrity outcomes across different contexts?

2

Mechanisms: Through what causal or associative pathways do these strategies produce their documented effects?

3

Transferability: Under what conditions can strategies proven in one context be successfully adapted to others?

02 ‚Äî Methodology

A six-stage API-driven workflow

The protocol follows PRISMA-P 2015 guidelines with a SPIDER analytical framework. Search and harvesting are fully automated; screening, extraction, and synthesis remain human-led.

01

Search Strategy

Configure and pilot-test database-specific Boolean queries. Validate syntax against each API before execution.

02

Data Harvesting

Automated retrieval across 8 databases via REST APIs. Rate-limited, error-handled, and fully logged.

03

Consolidation

Merge results, deduplicate using 50 % keyword-overlap matching, normalise metadata, validate completeness.

04

Screening

Title/abstract then full-text review. Human-led with conservative peer-review filtering at every stage.

05

Data Extraction

Structured JSON extraction. Risk of bias via Cochrane ROB-2. GRADE-CERQual confidence ratings applied.

06

Synthesis & Report

Thematic synthesis integrating quantitative and qualitative evidence. PRISMA-compliant manuscript output.

Conservative Peer-Review Filtering

Each source has explicit classification rules. Preprint repositories (arXiv, SSRN, ResearchGate), theses, and working papers are auto-excluded. Venues that cannot be verified against DOAJ, Scopus, or Web of Science are treated as ambiguous and excluded ‚Äî erring on the side of rigour.

Simple matching outperforms complex algorithms

Keyword overlap at a 50 % threshold achieved 42.3 % retrieval. Jaccard similarity at 80 % achieved only 3.8 %. Content-focused matching proved most robust against the title variations and punctuation differences common in academic metadata.
